A modern high-efficiency condensing boiler lasts **18 to 22 years** on average, and an older cast-iron boiler can run **25 to 30 years**. But those are national numbers, and Calgary isn't average. Our hard water and our freeze-thaw winters are hard on boilers, and in practice we see Calgary boilers reaching the end of their useful life earlier than the manufacturer's figure suggests — sometimes by several years.

The number on the spec sheet assumes clean water, annual servicing, and a correct install. Change any of those and the real-world lifespan moves. Here's what actually decides how long your boiler lasts, and how to tell when yours is near the end.

## What determines a boiler's lifespan

Age is the headline, but four things underneath it do the real work:

- **Water quality.** This is the big one in Calgary, and we come back to it below. Scale from hard water is the single biggest reason boilers here fail early.
- **Annual servicing.** A boiler that gets a proper [annual service](/boiler-service-calgary/) — combustion check, condensate trap cleaned, expansion tank verified, system flushed — routinely outlasts an identical unit that never gets touched. Skipped maintenance is the second most common reason we're called out to a boiler that died young.
- **Boiler type.** Cast-iron boilers are simple and heavy and last a long time, but they're inefficient. Modern condensing boilers are far more efficient but more complex, with more components that can fail. That's the trade: longevity versus efficiency.
- **Install quality.** A boiler sized wrong for the house, or plumbed and vented poorly, works harder and wears faster its entire life. A properly sized, properly commissioned [install](/boiler-installation-calgary/) is the foundation everything else sits on.

## Why Calgary is hard on boilers

If your boiler has failed earlier than you expected, this is almost always why — and it usually isn't the brand or bad luck.

**Hard water is the main culprit.** Calgary's water runs 150–200 mg/L of dissolved minerals. When water is heated, those minerals drop out of solution and bake onto the hottest surface in the system: the heat exchanger. That scale layer insulates the exchanger, so the boiler has to fire longer and hotter to push the same heat into your home. It wastes gas, it stresses the metal, and over years it's what cracks or corrodes the most expensive component in the boiler. A [water softener](/water-softener/) upstream is the single most effective thing you can do to extend a boiler's life in this city — it treats the cause rather than fighting the symptom every year.

**Chinook freeze-thaw adds thermal stress.** Calgary can swing from −25°C to +10°C and back within a day or two. Every one of those swings makes the boiler and its piping expand and contract, and repeated thermal cycling is fatiguing to heat exchangers, seals, and expansion tanks. It's the same reason our roads crack — it's just happening inside your mechanical room.

**A long heating season means more run hours.** Calgary's heating season runs roughly seven months. A boiler here simply does more work per year than the same unit in a milder climate, and more run hours means faster wear. The lifespan clock ticks faster here.

## Signs your boiler is near the end

A boiler rarely dies without warning — it tells you first. Watch for:

- **It's past 18 years.** On its own, age isn't a reason to replace a boiler that's running fine. But past 18 years the odds shift, and it's worth planning rather than waiting to be caught cold.
- **Rising gas bills with no change in habits.** A scaled, tired boiler burns more fuel to deliver the same heat. If your bills are climbing and your thermostat habits haven't, the boiler's efficiency is fading.
- **Repairs are getting frequent — or expensive.** One repair on a mid-life boiler is bad luck. The third call in two years is the boiler telling you something. And once a repair approaches a serious fraction of a new unit's cost — heat exchanger work especially — you're paying a lot to keep an old system limping.
- **Kettling — a rumbling or banging like a kettle.** That's scale on the heat exchanger trapping water and letting it flash to steam. Caught early it's a [flush and descale](/boiler-repair-calgary/); left for years it can crack the exchanger, at which point it's replacement, not repair.
- **Losing pressure repeatedly, or short-cycling.** Both can be minor fixes, but on an aging boiler they're often symptoms of a system wearing out. We cover both in detail in our guides on [why boilers lose pressure](/blog/boiler-losing-pressure-causes/) and [why a boiler keeps turning off](/blog/why-does-my-boiler-keep-turning-off/).

## Repair or replace?

The honest framework we use with Calgary homeowners comes down to age, cost, and safety:

- **Under about 12 years with a single failed part** — repair it. The boiler has years left and replacing it would throw away a working system.
- **Over 18–20 years with a major failure** — strongly consider [replacement](/boiler-installation-calgary/). You'd be putting an expensive component into a boiler whose other original parts are all the same age, and the next failure is coming.
- **A cracked heat exchanger, at any age** — replace. That's a safety issue, not a cost calculation, and it's the one item on this list with nothing to weigh.

The best thing you can do for whatever boiler you have is give it clean water and an annual service. Do both, and a Calgary boiler will get to the top of its range instead of the bottom.
